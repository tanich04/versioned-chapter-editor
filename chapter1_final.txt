## Chapter 1: The Canoe Builder

Dick stood on a jagged coral outcrop, surveying the southern horizon. Behind him, the ocean surged, flinging salty spray into the wind. Ahead lay the Karolin lagoon, a shimmering expanse of tranquility. Fishing gulls circled and called above its glassy surface. Forty miles of coral reef embraced this vast pool, creating a self-contained sea – a tempest in a high wind, a sapphire lake under calm skies.

And, as of yesterday, it was *his.*

The beach teemed with life: women weaving nets and tending gardens, children’s laughter echoing in the air, young men honing their skills. These were his people now. Sleek outrigger canoes lined the sand, ready for the water. Empty canoe houses stood silent, ghosts of a time when war canoes waited for battle.

But as Dick's gaze shifted from the serene lagoon to the vacant canoe houses, a shadow flickered across his face. He turned his back on the idyllic scene, facing the crashing waves and the open expanse of the northern sea. Somewhere beyond the horizon, invisible yet ever-present in his mind, lay Palm Tree Island. Beautiful, yes, achingly so, but a beauty that masked a festering nightmare.

Little Tari, son of Le Taioi, the net maker, sat nearby on the coral, watching him with wide, curious eyes. Tari might not understand the weight of leadership, but he knew Karolin was vulnerable. War had decimated the men, leaving the women, boys, and children defenseless, adrift without a guiding hand.

Then, *yesterday*, a strange outrigger had appeared from the north. Aboard were Katafa, a woman lost at sea years ago, and *him* – this enigmatic figure. *Sent by the gods,* the women whispered, to be their chief, their protector.

The boy didn't concern himself with gods. Alone with this intriguing stranger, and beyond the reach of his mother's scolding ear, he decided to get straight to the point.

"Taori," Tari piped up, tilting his head. "Who are you?" (é kamina tai)

How could Dick even *begin* to answer that question? And would Tari, so young, even grasp the weight of it? *Tari,* he could say, *I come from a world beyond your wildest imaginings. My name *used* to be Dick Lestrange. Even younger than you, I was shipwrecked with an old sailor, Kearney, on that island you call Marua – Palm Tree. We survived there, fished, and Kearney carved me toys – miniature ships, not like your graceful canoes. Then, Katafa arrived, swept away from Karolin by a storm. We lived as a family until Kearney passed, and then it was just the two of us. She taught me your language, and that’s when they started calling me Taori. We fell in love, and might have remained on Marua forever, but a ship came – a ship bearing cruel men from the Melanesian islands. They came for timber, but they brought mutiny and bloodshed, killing the white men and setting the ship ablaze. We barely escaped in our dinghy, salvaging what we could, even my little ships. We navigated by the lagoon light, guided home to Karolin.*

But he *couldn't* unleash that flood of memories on Tari. The name "Dick" was a phantom, a faded inscription on a tombstone. The English of his childhood had become a distant echo, replaced by the fluid tongue of Karolin. Kearney, the man who had raised him, was fading into the shimmering heat of the past. For the shipwrecked, the past was a blurred landscape. For Dick, his real life had begun with Katafa's arrival on Marua. Everything before was consumed by the relentless sun, the battering storms, the clinging mists of the rainy seasons. Kearney threatened to disappear entirely, save for those miniature ships, the only tangible links to a boyhood he could barely recall.

He looked down at the inquisitive child. "I am Taori, Tari tatu. Why do you ask?"

"I don't know," the boy shrugged, his attention already wavering. "I just ask. But no one important ever answers Tari's questions." A flash of insight crossed Dick's mind: *He's not wrong*. "Ai, the fish!" His easily distracted mind had latched onto a new excitement – the shouts of other children hauling in a fishing net. He was gone, scampering down the beach, forgotten question in tow.

Dick turned back to the north. The child's innocent question had opened a dam in his mind. He saw the schooner ablaze on the beach of Palm Tree, the faces of the brutal Melanesian sailors. He saw Katafa and himself, fleeing for their lives in the dinghy Kearney had taught him to handle. He saw their arrival here yesterday, the faces of the women and children, their hope-filled eyes seeing him as a godsend, their new leader.

But as he stared north, the memory of the men they had escaped tainted the beauty of the sea and sky.

There was no immediate threat. The men on Palm Tree had no canoes. *Not yet.* But they would build them. They *would* see the distant glow of the Karolin lagoon against the night sky, just as he had. And they would come. It might be weeks, months, but they would come.

He was almost a native now, a Kanaka, a savage, or so he liked to think. And yet, the white man remained, buried deep within him. He could think ahead, anticipate, strategize. He had already dispatched a canoe to the southern beach to summon Aioma, Palia, and Tafata – elders, past their fighting prime, but master canoe builders, their knowledge essential. He saw the potential in the tribe: nearly a hundred young men ripening into warriors. But beneath it all, driving him, was his profound love for Katafa, his other half, more precious than his own life. She was the one they threatened.

War canoes. Did he intend to defend the lagoon, to meet them on the beach? Or did he harbor a more audacious plan? A preemptive strike, to extinguish the threat before it ignited? Only time would tell.

A hand rested on his shoulder. He turned to find Katafa. A strand of her dark hair had escaped the vine holding it in place, fluttering in the breeze like an eagle's feather. Her dark, luminous eyes were fixed on the same point he had been scrutinizing – the northern horizon.

"Look," she said, her voice hushed.

At certain times, under the right atmospheric conditions, Palm Tree, though far beyond normal visibility, appeared in a mirage. They had witnessed it last evening. Now, it was happening again. A faint stain, a dark smudge, hovered low in the northern sky. It grew, sharpened, as if an unseen hand was sketching it into existence. The island rose from the sea, beautiful as a dream, both ethereal and strikingly real.

With her hand on his shoulder, they stood in silence, their minds uncluttered by science or understanding of atmospheric phenomena. They only knew they were gazing at the place they had fled, now rising up as if to haunt them.

They saw again the horde of desperate men on the beach, their faces distorted by rum and hunger, monstrous figures from a nightmare. They felt the wind filling their makeshift sail, propelling their little dinghy towards safety, towards the vast expanse of the open sea. They heard the drunken shouts of the Melanesians, fueled by stolen liquor. And there, before them, was the scene of the tragedy, a picture-postcard-perfect view that concealed the demons who still lurked there.

As Dick gazed at the beautiful illusion that was also a threat, his nostrils flared. His eyes darkened with a cold fury. They had threatened him, and now, they were threatening Katafa.

They *would* come. He felt it in his bones. The apparition of Palm Tree confirmed what instinct already told him. They would build canoes. They would see the lagoon light in the sky. They had no women, those men. And here were women. Just as he had been drawn to Katafa, these men would be drawn to the women of Karolin. They would scan the horizon, searching for an island to raid. And seeing the lagoon light, they would come.

But danger wasn't confined to the north. It lurked wherever greed, lust, or malice could fester. Not just among so-called savages, but also among the predators of civilization.

To Dick, there was no world beyond the waters embracing these two islands. No Europe, no America, no history save for the history of his own short life, and the life of Katafa. And yet, even in that brief span, he had learned to fear the darker aspects of men. He had glimpsed the underlying pattern of all history: humanity's instinct for war, for plunder, for destruction.

Gradually, the mirage of Palm Tree began to dissipate, vanishing like a candle snuffed out. As they turned from the sea to the lagoon, Katafa gestured toward a canoe approaching from the southern beach.

It was the canoe Dick had dispatched for the canoe builders. They left the coral outcrop and walked down to the white sand to greet them.
